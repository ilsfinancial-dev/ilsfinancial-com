How Fractional Commute Structure Changes Where You Can Live — and What State Taxes You Owe

Domicile, Gateway, and State Tax Residency for Fractional Pilots

Matt Samson, MBA, CPWA®  |  Marine Corps Veteran  |  ILS Financial

A Part 121 pilot has a crew base. A fractional pilot has a gateway. The two words sound similar. They do not produce the same planning problem.

Where you live drives what you owe.
Where you commute drives where you can live.

The Gateway Commute Model

Fractional operators position pilots from a designated commercial airport to the aircraft on the first day of each tour, and return them to that gateway at the end of the tour. Both NetJets and Flexjet pay for commercial transportation between the gateway and the aircraft. The aircraft moves; the pilot reports to a gateway, not a base.

This is structurally different from Part 121 commuting. A regional or major-carrier pilot whose crew base is Atlanta is responsible for getting to Atlanta in time for show. The pilot can live in Florida, but every trip starts with an out-of-pocket commute leg. A failure to commute is a failure to report.

In a fractional rotation, the operator absorbs the economic burden of positioning. The financial planning consequence is significant: the pilot can live almost anywhere with reasonable commercial air service and not pay for it out of pocket.

What This Unlocks for State Residency

Removing the commute-cost penalty turns state of residence into a genuine planning variable. Nine U.S. states impose no individual income tax on wage income: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. For a fractional pilot, many of these states can be operationally feasible depending on gateway access and personal circumstances.

A Part 121 pilot whose seniority anchors them to a high-tax state crew base can face a substantial cumulative state tax burden over a career. The same pilot at a fractional operator can often restructure that exposure through a properly executed change of residence.

Important Distinction

Operationally easy is not the same as legally complete. State revenue departments are aware that fractional pilots can move easily, and the burden of proof on the taxpayer is correspondingly real. A residency change must be executed — not just asserted.

Domicile vs. Statutory Residency

Two separate legal concepts govern state residency for income tax purposes, and both matter for fractional pilots.

Domicile

The place a taxpayer treats as a permanent home and intends to return to. Establishing a new domicile requires both physical presence and demonstrable intent. Courts and revenue departments evaluate intent through voter registration, driver's license, vehicle registration, location of family, location of professional advisors, location of banking and social affiliations, and patterns of personal time spent.

Statutory Residency

A separate test used by many high-tax states. New York, for example, taxes any individual who maintains a permanent place of abode in the state and is physically present there for more than 183 days in a tax year as a resident — regardless of domicile. A taxpayer can change domicile and still be taxed as a statutory resident if they retain a home and spend enough days in the former state.

Audit Pressure From Former States

State revenue departments have systematically increased residency-audit activity over the past decade. A clean change of residence is structural work, not a paperwork exercise. Commonly evaluated steps include:

  • Complete a state-by-state inventory of contacts — driver's license, voter registration, vehicle, dependents, primary care physician, banking, club memberships — and shift the dominant share to the new state.
  • File a final part-year resident return in the former state with a clearly identified change-of-residence date.
  • Document day counts contemporaneously through credit card receipts, cellular location data, or a dedicated tracker. Reconstructing days from memory rarely satisfies an auditor.
  • Review real-property holdings carefully. A retained vacation home in the former state is one of the most common audit evidence points used to assert continued domicile.
  • Update estate documents — wills, revocable trusts, powers of attorney — to reflect the new state of domicile, and confirm the choice-of-law and venue language.
  • Confirm W-2 withholding state with the operator after the residency change. A mismatched address or stale W-4 can produce nonresident-state filings that attract audit attention even when the substantive position is correct.

How This Fits the ILS Decision Sequencing System™

State residency is evaluated as a tax-bucket sequencing question. For fractional pilots, the leverage is higher than for most W-2 earners — residency is one of the few decisions that compounds for the entire balance of a career and can only be reset with deliberate work.

A fractional pilot working through the sequence should generally:

  • 1

    Confirm the operator's gateway list and the practical commercial air service options from prospective new states.

  • 2

    Identify the tax delta between current and prospective states using current-year withholding and projected production pay.

  • 3

    Cross-check the residency change against spousal employment, dependents, and real-estate constraints.

  • 4

    Sequence the residency change to align with a clean tax-year boundary where possible.

  • 5

    Update beneficiary forms, estate documents, and W-4 to reflect the new state of domicile.

Planning Priority

Allocation choices made before the residency picture is settled tend to be re-done. Sequencing the residency decision first preserves the option set. Planning structure is typically addressed before allocation decisions.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What is a "gateway" in fractional aviation?

    A gateway is the commercial airport where a fractional pilot begins and ends each tour. Unlike a traditional airline crew base, the operator typically arranges and pays for transportation between the gateway and the aircraft — giving pilots greater flexibility in choosing where to live without absorbing a commute cost.

  • How is a gateway different from an airline crew base?

    A crew base requires the pilot to commute at their own expense and responsibility — a failure to get there is a failure to report. A gateway shifts the positioning responsibility to the operator. This structural difference is what makes state residency a genuine planning variable for fractional pilots in a way it typically is not for Part 121 pilots.

  • Can fractional pilots live in any state?

    In many cases, fractional pilots can live in a wide range of states with reasonable commercial air service. Practical considerations — flight availability, schedule reliability, personal circumstances, and family logistics — still influence viable choices, but the constraint is narrower than for a Part 121 pilot anchored to a specific crew base.

  • Do fractional pilots have to pay state income tax where they work?

    Federal law (49 U.S.C. §40116) generally limits state taxation of qualifying air carrier compensation to the pilot's state of residence and, in some cases, a state where a majority of income is earned. Application of these rules depends on specific facts and should be evaluated with a qualified tax professional.

  • What is the difference between domicile and tax residency?

    Domicile refers to a taxpayer's permanent home and intended long-term residence. Statutory residency is a separate test used by some states, based on factors such as days spent in the state and maintaining a residence there. A taxpayer can be domiciled in one state but treated as a resident of another under a statutory residency test — which is why simply changing domicile is not always sufficient.

  • How do states determine domicile?

    States evaluate domicile through multiple factors simultaneously — no single factor controls:

    • Driver's license and voter registration location
    • Location of family and primary residence
    • Banking, professional, and legal relationships
    • Time spent in each location over the year
    • Location of safe deposit boxes, club memberships, and religious affiliations

    States assess the overall pattern of behavior, not any one indicator.

  • What is the 183-day rule?

    Some states, notably New York, treat an individual as a tax resident if they maintain a permanent place of abode in the state and spend more than 183 days there during the tax year — regardless of where the individual claims domicile. This rule can apply even to a pilot whose legal domicile is in a no-income-tax state, if they retain a home in New York and spend enough days there.

  • Does being on the road reduce residency risk?

    Time spent traveling can reduce days counted in a specific state, which may help in meeting statutory residency thresholds. However, days spent in a former state — especially at a retained property — still count. The fractional schedule's time-away-from-home pattern helps on day counts, but does not eliminate the need to track carefully.

  • Why are residency audits a concern for fractional pilots?

    Because fractional pilots have structural flexibility in where they live, state tax authorities may scrutinize residency changes more closely than they would for a typical W-2 worker. The burden of proof is on the taxpayer to demonstrate a valid change of domicile — a clean residency change requires documented, contemporaneous evidence, not a reconstructed narrative.

  • What documentation supports a change of domicile?

    Common supporting evidence includes:

    • Updated driver's license and voter registration in the new state
    • Relocation of primary residence and termination of prior residence
    • Banking, professional, and legal relationships moved to the new state
    • Consistent use of the new address across financial and legal documents
    • Contemporaneous day-count tracking (calendar, receipts, device location data)

  • Does owning property in a former state create risk?

    Yes. Retaining a home in a former state is one of the most common evidence points used to assert continued domicile or to support a statutory residency position. The impact depends on how the property is used, how many days are spent there, and the other surrounding facts — but it is a material factor in any residency audit.

  • How should pilots track time in different states?

    Common methods include calendar logs, travel records, credit card receipts, and mobile device location data. Accurate, contemporaneous records are substantially more reliable in an audit than reconstructed estimates. Many taxpayers who lose residency audits do so on documentation, not on the substantive merits of their position.

  • Can changing states reduce taxes for fractional pilots?

    In some cases, a change in state of residence may reduce overall state tax exposure. The outcome depends on income structure, state laws, the specific execution of the residency change, and whether any prior-state ties trigger continued taxation. The potential benefit compounds over a career, which is why it warrants careful evaluation early.

  • When should a residency change occur?

    Many individuals align residency changes with a clean tax-year boundary to simplify reporting and avoid part-year complexity in both states. Timing also depends on income patterns, the pilot's schedule, and personal logistics such as dependent enrollment and spousal employment.

  • Where does residency fit in the ILS financial planning sequence for fractional pilots?

    Residency is evaluated as part of the tax-bucket sequencing phase. Because it affects multiple downstream decisions — withholding, investment account siting, estate document jurisdiction, beneficiary designations — it is typically addressed before making long-term allocation decisions. Settling the residency picture first prevents rework.

  • Does changing residency affect employer payroll and withholding?

    Yes. After a residency change, payroll records and W-4 withholding should reflect the new state. Mismatches between the residence of record and the withholding state can produce nonresident-state filings that attract audit scrutiny even when the substantive tax position is correct. Confirm the update with the operator's payroll department after a residency change.

  • Is changing domicile a simple process?

    No. Establishing a new domicile requires both physical presence in the new state and demonstrated intent to make it a permanent home. The process involves coordinated updates across legal, financial, and personal records — and the evidence must be contemporaneous, not reconstructed after the fact.

  • Should pilots consult professionals before changing residency?

    Residency decisions involve tax, legal, and financial considerations that interact with each other. Many individuals consult a qualified tax professional for the state-law analysis, an estate attorney for document updates, and a financial advisor for the downstream planning implications. Coordinating these advisors around the same decision avoids gaps.
